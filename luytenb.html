ABOUT

Luyten B was discovered as an exoplanet in the system of Luyten Star (GJ 273), located about 12.4 light-years from Earth. Discovered in 2017, it was detected by infrared and optical observations. In particular, the high-precision spectrographs used by the European Southern Observatory (ESO) played an important role in this discovery. Luyten B was detected by a highly sensitive spectrometer called HARPS (High Accuracy Radial velocity Planet Searcher) because of its motion around its star. HARPS is a system that helps determine the presence of planets by measuring their gravitational effects.

Luyten B orbits Luyten Star (GJ 273), a cold and small star. The Luyten Star is known as an M-class red dwarf and is much less bright and massive than the Sun. Such stars are generally long-lived and emit low energy, which can increase the habitable potential of the planets around them. The surface temperature of Luyten's star is about 3,400 Kelvin, making it a relatively low-temperature star. Luyten B is thought to be located in the habitable zone of this star, that is, at a distance where liquid water could exist.

The radius of Luyten B is not known exactly, but it is estimated to be between about 1.1 and 1.5 Earth radii. These values indicate that the planet is similar in size to Earth. The mass of the planet is calculated to be about 2.9 times that of Earth. This leads to its classification as a super-Earth. Besides its mass and location in the potentially habitable zone, there is no precise information about its atmospheric composition, but research suggests that Luyten B's atmospheric conditions may allow for the presence of liquid water.
